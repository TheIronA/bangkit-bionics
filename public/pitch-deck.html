Southeast Asia is a 695 million-person region, yet access to assistive products remains highly unequal. WHO estimates that more than 2.5 billion people globally need one or more assistive products, and cost remains one of the biggest barriers to access.

Why Now

Three technology curves
converged at once.

Hardware

3D Printing Matured

Industrial FDM printers now produce biocompatible-grade nylon and TPU parts at <RM 200 material cost per arm. This was not possible at this price point before 2022. Infill, wall thickness, and joint geometry can be tuned per patient in hours, not weeks.

Electronics

EMG Sensors Commoditised

Surface electromyography (EMG) sensors that once cost USD 2,000 per channel now retail for under USD 20. Microcontrollers with sufficient processing power for pattern recognition cost under USD 5. The electronics BOM for a myoelectric prosthetic is now under RM 400.

Intelligence

ML on Microcontrollers

TinyML frameworks (TensorFlow Lite, Edge Impulse) enable gesture classification models to run on RM 15 microcontrollers with <5ms latency. Intent recognition that required cloud compute in 2020 now runs entirely on-device. No internet needed.

Policy

Medical Device Route Exists

Malaysia already has an active medical-device regulatory framework through MDA. That gives us a concrete pathway to plan around, even though final device classification still has to be confirmed with the regulator.

Market Gap

Regional Scale, Uneven Access

A 695 million-person region with uneven access to assistive care creates room for a locally built, serviceable product. Our bet is that Malaysia can be the beachhead for that model.

Technology

How the arm works.

01

Muscle Signal Capture

Surface EMG electrodes rest against residual limb skin. When the patient intends to grip, forearm muscles produce millivolt-level electrical signals. No surgery. No skin penetration.

02

Amplification & Filtering

Custom analogue front-end amplifies the signal 1,000× and applies a bandpass filter (20–500 Hz) to extract clean myoelectric data. Hardware noise rejection ensures reliable outdoor use.

03

Neural-Pattern Grip Classification

A TinyML model running on a low-power ARM Cortex-M4 microcontroller classifies signal patterns into grip intentions: power grasp, pinch, tripod, hook, open hand. The goal is more intuitive control than threshold-only systems.

04

Motorized Grip Switching

Classified intent drives miniaturised servo motors in the fingers and planned grip-state switching in the thumb. The objective is automatic pinch and grasp changes without manual repositioning by the other hand.

05

Patient Training & Digital-Twin Fit

Companion software guides patients through EMG calibration and socket personalisation. The goal is a higher-comfort fit model than standard measurement-to-socket workflows, with rapid re-training and re-printing over time.

Bill of Materials (Prototype Scale)

  • Structure: FDM-printed biocompatible nylon (PA12) — RM 180
  • Actuators: 5× micro servo motors — RM 120
  • EMG front-end: Custom PCB + electrodes — RM 85
  • MCU + BLE: Nordic nRF52840 module — RM 45
  • Power: Li-ion 2000mAh + USB-C charge IC — RM 55
  • Cosmetic silicone glove: Custom moulded — RM 90
  • Assembly + QC labour: RM 200–400
Total prototype COGS: RM 775–975 direct BOM only; fully loaded pilot COGS lands at RM 2,500–3,500 once fitting, QA, clinician support, replacements, and warranty reserve are included.

IP & Defensibility

  • Provisional patent on EMG control system architecture
  • Proprietary patient dataset for gesture model training
  • Custom socket-interface connector (mechanical IP)
  • MDA registration as regulatory moat in Malaysia
