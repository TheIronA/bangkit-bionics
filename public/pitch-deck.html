The Problem

695 million people live in SEA.
Access to assistive care is still uneven.

Southeast Asia is a 695 million-person region, yet access to assistive products remains highly unequal. WHO estimates that more than 2.5 billion people globally need one or more assistive products, and cost remains one of the biggest barriers to access.

WHO also reports that access can be as low as 3% in some low-income countries, compared with 90% in some high-income countries. In Southeast Asia and other low- and middle-income countries, industrial accidents and road traffic injuries — not disease — are the primary driver of upper-limb loss in working-age adults.

Why Now

Three technology curves
converged at once.

Hardware

3D Printing Matured

Industrial FDM printers now produce biocompatible-grade nylon and TPU parts at <RM 200 material cost per arm. This was not possible at this price point before 2022. Infill, wall thickness, and joint geometry can be tuned per patient in hours, not weeks.

Electronics

EMG Sensors Commoditised

Surface electromyography (EMG) sensors that once cost USD 2,000 per channel now retail for under USD 20. Microcontrollers with sufficient processing power for pattern recognition cost under USD 5. The electronics BOM for a myoelectric prosthetic is now under RM 400.

Intelligence

ML on Microcontrollers

TinyML frameworks (TensorFlow Lite, Edge Impulse) enable gesture classification models to run on RM 15 microcontrollers with <5ms latency. Intent recognition that required cloud compute in 2020 now runs entirely on-device. No internet needed.

Policy

Medical Device Route Exists

Malaysia already has an active medical-device regulatory framework through MDA. That gives us a concrete pathway to plan around, even though final device classification still has to be confirmed with the regulator.

Market Gap

Regional Scale, Uneven Access

A 695 million-person region with uneven access to assistive care creates room for a locally built, serviceable product. Our bet is that Malaysia can be the beachhead for that model.

Technology

How the arm works.

01

FMG + EMG Sensor Fusion

Two complementary sensor types capture muscle intent from the residual limb: EMG electrodes pick up millivolt-level electrical signals; FMG sensors read pressure changes as muscles contract. Peer-reviewed research shows combined FMG+EMG achieves >97% classification accuracy under impeded-contact conditions (sweat, clothing isolation) versus ~84–91% for EMG alone — a meaningful gap for a device worn in tropical Southeast Asia (Ke et al., Sensors, 2020; doi:10.3390/s20174775). Both are non-invasive, wearable, and low-cost. No surgery. No skin penetration.

02

Signal Conditioning

Custom analogue front-end amplifies EMG 1,000× with bandpass filtering (20–500 Hz). FMG channels are independently conditioned. Combined multi-modal signal processing extracts clean data reliable in outdoor and humid conditions.

03

On-Device Neural Net Classification

Stage one uses threshold-based control: the signal pipeline is demonstrated. Stage two — in development — trains a compact neural network on the patient’s own FMG+EMG data to classify 4–6 grip intentions directly on the microcontroller. No cloud dependency, no latency. Transfer learning adapts the model to new users and accommodates residual limb changes over time, reducing per-patient calibration overhead. The claim is not that this matches Ottobock’s decade of R&D. It is that it covers the grips most users need, runs on a RM 45 MCU, and works reliably in day-to-day use.

04

Motorized Grip Architecture

Target prototype design: motorized grip switching using a 5-servo layout that covers the three grip states used in 80%+ of ADL tasks (power grasp, pinch, open). Manual thumb repositioning — the most-cited complaint against Vulcan-class devices — is eliminated by the design. Full independent per-finger actuation is a post-prototype iteration, not a Year-1 target.

05

EMG Calibration & Clinical Socket Fitting

Companion software guides the patient through EMG signal calibration. Separately, our clinical team performs 3D scanning of the residual limb to generate a patient-specific socket — no special hardware needed from the patient. Socket fitting is a clinical process, not a consumer task. Rapid re-calibration and re-printing over time as the residual limb changes.

Bill of Materials (Prototype Scale)

  • Structure: FDM-printed biocompatible nylon (PA12) — RM 180
  • Actuators: 5× micro servo motors — RM 120 — configured for 3 distinct grip states via coupled finger groups; independent per-finger actuation is a post-prototype target
  • FMG + EMG front-end: Custom PCB, FMG pressure sensors + EMG electrodes — RM 110
  • MCU + BLE: Nordic nRF52840 module — RM 45
  • Power: Li-ion 2000mAh + USB-C charge IC — RM 55
  • Cosmetic silicone glove: Custom moulded — RM 90
  • Assembly + QC labour: RM 200–400
Total prototype COGS: RM 800–1,000 direct BOM only; fully loaded pilot COGS lands at RM 2,500–3,500 once fitting, QA, clinician support, replacements, and warranty reserve are included.

IP & Defensibility

  • Provisional patent application in preparation — EMG control system architecture
  • Patient-specific FMG+EMG gesture dataset — collection commences in NTIS clinical trial; each patient adds to a proprietary multi-modal corpus competitors cannot replicate quickly
  • Transfer learning pipeline for per-patient model adaptation — reduces calibration time and enables model updates as residual limb changes over time
  • Custom socket-interface connector (mechanical IP)
  • MDA registration as regulatory moat in Malaysia
